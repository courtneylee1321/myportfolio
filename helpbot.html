Henry the Helpbot 2019

Desktop Chatbot for Onboarding Associates

Duration

3 months

Overview

Henry the HelpBot is a desktop app designed to help new onboarding associates find out more information about resources and help available to them. These resources can vary from asking about health benefits offered by the company to ask about how to request an accommodation through human resources.

Primary Goal:
Having a system that can give you immediate answers at your convenience

Users and Audience

Our users and audience consists of:

-Young associates that onboarded to the company within the past 1-5 years
-Interns recently onboarded for the summer

Project Statement

As a young professional, the challenge of navigating a new company is a crucial first step in their careers. Whether it be their first job out of college or an internship, learning how to collaborate with others and establishing steady professional relationships is a challenging aspect in any career.

The process of working in a new, fast-paced environment can be extremely overwhelming for those who have permanent disabilities. Our understanding of how the workplace can be more accommodating and inclusive is ambiguous.

Roles and Responsibilities

I worked on the project with another UX intern where we both:

-Conducted preliminary research identifying onboarding experiences of associates
-Spoke to an associate from HR and a career counselor specialized in individuals with autism
-Reached out to disability ERG chairs from different locations within company
-Researched existing resources both within the company and at other companies
-Created empathy maps, user personas, and prototypes
-Conducted survey questions, user research and interviews

  1. How we Decided on Social Anxiety

After reviewing our research, we realized that most participants experienced anxiety while onboarding to the company.

Whether it be these individuals are Hard of Hearing, have Asperger’s, Cerebral Palsy, etc, all of these individuals had difficulty adjusting to their new positions.

Across the board, the process of trying to ask for help was something associates found difficult.

  1. Researching Social Anxiety

We wanted to find out more about social anxiety and how it could be manifest in the workplace.

With this information, we could figure out what kind of solution could fit their needs the best.

We were fortunate enough to be able to talk to a mental health counselor specialized in young adults with autism and social anxiety.

These are the following tips she gave us:


-Set expectations from the start by saying “I’m here to help you at any point

-Encourage taking breaks

-Mentors are important to have in setting expectations and lowering fears

Notes about Social Anxiety

  1. POVS AND User Personas

Now that we had a better understanding of social anxiety, we started creating a POV and user persona to give a name and face to our user.

We had multiple drafts for our POV because we were still figuring out what problem we wanted to solve.

My partner and I found ourselves trying to solve too many problems in our POV.


POV Drafts

Final POV

Elijah, a 26 year-old young professional with social anxiety, has recently been hired.

He needs a way to comfortably ask for help.

This is important because he is afraid to embarrass himself in front of his colleagues while speaking to them.

This is currently a problem because the department teams do not consistently provide sufficient resources and guidance for new associates while onboarding

We finally narrowed down the problem we wanted to focus on was finding a way to comfortably ask for help.

We began ideating resources that could be introduced to new associates to make it easier for them to ask for help and be aware of their resources.

We found an existing onboarding site that we initially thought we could add it, but we found that it was too big.

My partner and I moved onto to drafting an accommodation site for those who have disabilities.

With screening for social anxiety, we asked associates how much they agreed/disagreed statements that are common with social anxiety.

  1. Usability Testing

Part 1: 30 Minute Interview


To better understand the onboarding process, we decided to conduct a 30 minute interview with participants from our screener.

The question that generated the most pain-points was:

If you could change anything about your onboarding experience, what would it be?



Part 2: 30 Minute Usability Testing Session


We showed participants the following paper prototype to the right and & got their initial impressions.

PIVOT


Without being informed about the purpose of the site being for associates that struggle with social anxiety, interviewees didn’t fully grasp the purpose of the site and associated with an existing site.

We revised our prototype and decided to focus on the virtual assistant part at towards the bottom of the prototype:

Paper prototype shown to participants after pivot



The virtual assistant would immediately allow an associate to ask a question and receive an answer without needing to ask anyone in person.

We realized that our accommodation website was not giving Elijah a clear way to ask for help.

  1. Prototyping

We created 3 examples of how Henry the Helpbot could used to improve onboarding baed on problems we identified during our user interviews.


Pre-Onboarding Scenario


Before Elijah comes for orientation, he will be emailed some instructions along with a link that provides them with access to Henry the Helpbot. From there, they can learn more about what’s going on to happen during orientation, get to know their team, and learn more about parking and transportation.


Onboarding Scenario

Elijah has just finished his orientation and was given information about the company, volunteer opportunities, health benefits, and more. Henry the Helpbot sends you the slide deck from orientation so that you can refer back to it any point.


Post-Onboarding Scenario

Elijah has been at the company for a few days and he received Agile training, but can’t remember all the different terms that were discussed. In this scenario, Elijah can’t remember what scrum master is so he decides to ask Henry. Henry gives him a definition and a link to more training that he could do online.


  1. Outcomes and Lessons

At the conclusion of our project, my partner and I were able to present higher fidelity wireframes with examples of conversations that would take place.

Although our design wasn’t able to be developed more, the backing for Henry the Helpbot is well supported through research.

Our project was focused on how and why Henry the Helpbot would be a useful application as opposed to the features of the application.

Next Steps for Project:

  1. Figure out features to incorporate within Henry the Helpbot
  2. Conduct the 2nd round of usability testing to validate/invalidate our use cases
  3. Work with managers and onboarding team to have their thoughts and suggestions

Lessons


This project showed me how important it is to iterate in order to make sure you truly have a full grasp on the scope of the problem you are trying to solve.

Our problem kept changing as we found more information about social anxiety & we ran into trouble focusing on one issue

One aspect I’d like to improve on is trying to avoid leading questions during interviews. I found it tough to make sure I wasn’t leading participants or interviewees to answer a certain way.
